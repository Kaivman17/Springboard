Hospital Readmissions Data Analysis and Recommendations for Reduction¶

Background¶

In October 2012, the US government's Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) began reducing Medicare payments for Inpatient Prospective Payment System hospitals with excess readmissions. Excess readmissions are measured by a ratio, by dividing a hospitals number of predicted 30-day readmissions for heart attack, heart failure, and pneumonia by the number that would be expected, based on an average hospital with similar patients. A ratio greater than 1 indicates excess readmissions.

Statistical Significance vs. Practical Significance¶

We acheived a statistically significant result in this experiment, however in this situation there is a difference between statistical significance and practical significance. Our statistical significance experiment using the null hypothesis told us that this difference in mean amount of discharges between hospitals that have a readmission ratio > 1 and hospitals that have a readmission ratio <= 1 is not due to sampling error. One major issue with observing statistical significance in large sample sizes is that almost any null hypothesis can be rejected purely due to the variance of a large sample size. Practical significance points to an effect size of a particular variable that has an impact on the question. We need to determine what the effect size is of the number of discharges on a hospital's readmission ratio.

In this situation there are more factors that can affect a hospital's readmission ratio than the the amount of discharges or hospital capacity. One huge factor that can be overlooked in this data is the location of each hospital. It would be fare to assume that hospitals that are in less populated areas of the country are not surrounded by many other hospitals. In this case, the readmission ratios for these hospitals are likely to be higher simply due to the fact that there are not many other hospitals in the area. On the other hand, hospitals that are located in more populated areas of the country could be surrounded by several other hospitals. These hospitals are likely to have a lower readmission ratio due to the wide variety of hospital choices in the area.

My recommendation after this analysis would be to change the second recommendation of providing incentives to consolidate hospitals and facilities. Instead I would recommend that hospitals that are in less populated areas should demonstrate the resources and ability to provide quality care for residents in the area and should be inspected more thoroughly as there are no other patient care options in the area. I would also reccomend learning more about why a patient was readmitted to a hospital as the answer to this question seems to be where a more impactful decision can be made.
